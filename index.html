📚 Theory of Projectile Motion

Understanding the physics behind the simulation

01

What is Projectile Motion?

Projectile motion is the motion of an object thrown or projected into the air, subject only to the acceleration of gravity. The object is called a projectile, and its path is called its trajectory.

The key insight is that the horizontal and vertical components of motion are independent of each other. Horizontal motion has constant velocity, while vertical motion is uniformly accelerated due to gravity.

02

Key Equations

Horizontal Position
x(t) = v₀ · cos(θ) · t
Vertical Position
y(t) = h₀ + v₀ · sin(θ) · t − ½ · g · t²
Horizontal Velocity
vₓ = v₀ · cos(θ) = constant
Vertical Velocity
vᵧ(t) = v₀ · sin(θ) − g · t
Maximum Height
H = h₀ + (v₀ · sin(θ))² / (2g)
Range (h₀ = 0)
R = v₀² · sin(2θ) / g
Time of Flight (h₀ = 0)
T = 2 · v₀ · sin(θ) / g
03

Assumptions & Conditions

  • ✦
    No Air Resistance — The only force acting on the projectile is gravity.
  • ✦
    Uniform Gravity — Gravitational acceleration (g) is constant throughout the motion.
  • ✦
    Flat Earth Approximation — The ground is assumed to be flat (no curvature effects).
  • ✦
    Point Mass — The projectile is treated as a point mass (no rotation or spin).
  • ✦
    Two-Dimensional Motion — Motion is confined to a vertical plane.
04

Special Cases & Insights

🎯

Maximum Range

For level ground (h₀ = 0), maximum range is achieved at θ = 45°.

🔄

Complementary Angles

Angles θ and 90° − θ give the same range on level ground.

⏱️

Symmetry of Time

Time ascending = Time descending (when launched and landing at same height).

📐

Independence

Horizontal and vertical motions are completely independent of each other.
